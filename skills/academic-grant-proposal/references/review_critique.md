## Mock-Reviewer Critique Workflow

Run before submission. Read the proposal **adopting a reviewer's mindset** — skeptical, time-constrained, scoring against a rubric.

### Procedure

1. **Print or read** the proposal in one sitting (~45 minutes for a 15 pp NSF, ~90 minutes for a 45 pp Horizon).
2. **Open the funder's published evaluation criteria** alongside.
3. **Score each criterion** as the reviewer would (1-5 NIH, 1-5 Horizon, 1-9 ERC) with one sentence of justification.
4. **List the top 5 weaknesses** you would write in a review.
5. **List the top 3 strengths** as the reviewer would phrase them.
6. **Predict the reject/revise/accept verdict.**
7. **Compare to a friendly external reviewer** if available — discrepancies highlight clarity issues.

### Calibration

If your self-score is uniformly above 4/5 (Horizon) or below 3 (NIH), recalibrate — proposals that look perfect to authors usually have blind spots. Aim for *honest* scores; act on the lowest one.

## Funder-Specific Rubric Checklists

### NSF Reviewer Checklist (Intellectual Merit + Broader Impacts)

**Intellectual Merit:**
- [ ] Is the activity advancing knowledge and understanding within the field, or across fields?
- [ ] How well-qualified is the proposer to conduct the project?
- [ ] To what extent does the project explore creative, original concepts?
- [ ] How well-conceived and -organized is the proposed activity?
- [ ] Are there sufficient resources at the home institution or through collaborations?

**Broader Impacts:**
- [ ] Does the activity benefit society or advance desired societal outcomes?
- [ ] Are the activities specific, measurable, and executable?
- [ ] Does the PI have a track record of broader-impact activities?
- [ ] Are appropriate beneficiaries explicitly identified?

### NIH Reviewer Checklist (Significance / Innovation / Approach + Investigator + Environment)

**Significance:**
- [ ] Does the project address an important problem in the field?
- [ ] Will it improve scientific knowledge / clinical practice / methodology?

**Innovation:**
- [ ] Are the concepts, approaches, instrumentation novel?
- [ ] Does the work refine, improve, or propose new theoretical concepts / approaches / methodologies?

**Approach:**
- [ ] Is the strategy well-reasoned and appropriate?
- [ ] Are potential problems and alternative strategies considered?
- [ ] Are preliminary data presented?

**Investigator(s):**
- [ ] Are the PIs well-suited to the project?
- [ ] Do they bring complementary expertise?

**Environment:**
- [ ] Will the institution contribute to project success?
- [ ] Are unique features available (collaborators, equipment, data)?

### ERC Reviewer Checklist

**Project (B1 + B2):**
- [ ] Ground-breaking nature, ambition, and feasibility
- [ ] High-gain / high-risk articulated
- [ ] Methodology adequate to the question

**PI:**
- [ ] Track record demonstrates capacity to produce ground-breaking work
- [ ] Independence from prior advisors (StG / CoG)
- [ ] Appropriate to grant tier (StG: emerging; CoG: consolidating; AdG: senior)

### Horizon Europe Reviewer Checklist (Excellence / Impact / Implementation)

**Excellence (1/3):**
- [ ] Objectives and ambition vs. SoTA quantified
- [ ] Methodology specific, justified, beyond SoTA
- [ ] Interdisciplinary and gender dimensions addressed

**Impact (1/3):**
- [ ] Pathways to expected outcomes and impacts
- [ ] Quantitative impact magnitude
- [ ] PCDE plan with specific KPIs

**Implementation (1/3):**
- [ ] WPs cohesive, deliverables and milestones realistic
- [ ] Effort matches budget
- [ ] Consortium complementarity, no gaps, no overlap

## Common Rejection Reasons

Aggregated across funders, in approximate frequency order:

1. **Weak motivation** — the problem is not convincingly important. Failure mode: "interesting but not significant."
2. **Insufficient innovation** — the work reads as incremental over prior work. Failure mode: "not enough beyond what is already known."
3. **Methodology gaps** — fragile design, missing controls, weak statistical plan, unrealistic compute.
4. **Aims dependent on each other** — failure of Aim 1 invalidates Aims 2-3.
5. **Missing or unfair baselines** in CS evaluation plans.
6. **Preliminary data missing** (NIH especially), or preliminary data does not support the proposed direction.
7. **Risks naïve** — "no risks identified" or unrealistic mitigations.
8. **Broader Impact / Impact treated as boilerplate** — generic statements not linked to specific beneficiaries.
9. **Team mismatch** — Aims attributed to people without demonstrated relevant expertise.
10. **Budget mismatch** — work plan describes 3 postdocs, budget shows 1; or compute under-justified.
11. **Length / format violations** — exceeds page or word limits, missing headers, wrong template.
12. **Recycled text** — proposal reads as repurposed from a different mechanism.
13. **Outdated literature** — citations stop 3+ years before submission in fast-moving fields.
14. **Vague milestones** — "we will improve performance" without quantitative target or verification.

## Top 10 Self-Critique Questions

Run these before finalizing:

1. Can I state the central hypothesis in one sentence?
2. Does each Aim / Objective have a clear, testable hypothesis?
3. Is each Aim independent — would the work proceed if any single Aim fails?
4. For every numerical claim in the proposal, can I point to its source?
5. Are baselines named (not "we compare against the SOTA")?
6. Does the budget match the work plan exactly?
7. Have I quantified at least one Broader Impact / Impact metric?
8. Is each risk paired with a concrete, **already-prepared** mitigation?
9. Could a reviewer outside my exact subfield understand the contribution?
10. Have I read the **current** call/solicitation in full and addressed every required section?

## Response-to-Reviewers (Resubmission)

For resubmissions, reviewers expect **substantive revision** plus an explicit response document.

### Universal structure

1. **Cover Letter / Introduction** (1 page typical): summarize how the proposal has been strengthened
2. **Per-criticism response**: cite the reviewer's exact concern, then describe the revision
3. **Visual diff if helpful**: bold/italic to indicate text added; reference page/section in revised proposal
4. **Summary table**: criticism → response → location in revised proposal

### Tone

- Acknowledge each comment respectfully
- Do not argue: even when the reviewer is wrong, respond by clarifying the proposal
- Concrete revisions; not promises
- Concede valid weaknesses gracefully

## NIH A1 Resubmission

NIH allows **one resubmission (A1)** within 37 months of original submission. Format:

- **Introduction page**: 1 page, prefixed to the Research Strategy
- Address the **summary statement** comments — reviewers explicitly look for this
- Highlight changes in the body using **bracketed markup** (e.g., bold or vertical line in margin) — current NIH practice does NOT require markup but reviewers appreciate it
- Resubmissions that do not engage with prior reviews score poorly

### Introduction page structure

```
Paragraph 1: Brief overview (~3 lines) — restate the project's central thesis and that it has been substantially revised.

Paragraph 2-N: Address each reviewer concern by category:
   "Reviewer 1, Concern 1 (Approach)": [concern, paraphrased] → [response, with section pointer]
   "Reviewer 2, Concern 1 (Innovation)": ...

Closing paragraph: Confirm responsiveness; thank the panel.
```

Stay within 1 page. Use full sentences, not bullet lists, for NIH style.

## NSF Resubmission

NSF does not have a formal A1 mechanism — a resubmitted proposal is a new submission. However, panels remember (especially CISE no-deadline programs).

- **Address prior panel feedback proactively** in the new submission
- Often included in **Results from Prior NSF Support** section (if PI had NSF funding) or as an **introductory paragraph**
- A short "What changed since previous submission" paragraph at the start of Project Description signals seriousness
- Avoid sounding apologetic — restate the strengthened argument

## ERC Resubmission

ERC has a **cooling-off** mechanism:

- Score "C" at Step 1 → **2-year ban** on ERC applications
- Score "B" at Step 2 → **1-year ban**
- Otherwise: free to resubmit, must substantially revise

ERC does not have a formal response document — submit a revised B1 + B2. The revised proposal may reference how it has evolved, but the bulk of the work is in the science itself.

## Horizon Resubmission

Horizon proposals receive an **Evaluation Summary Report (ESR)** with per-criterion scoring and comments. Resubmission to a later call:

- Read the ESR systematically; address each weakness
- A revised proposal may reference the ESR comments in a brief intro, but most response is via revised content
- If the score was just below threshold (e.g., 13.5/15), targeted revision often succeeds
- If a criterion failed threshold (<3.0/5), substantial restructure needed

## Tone and Phrase Templates for Responses

### Acknowledging a valid criticism
> "We thank the reviewer for raising this concern. The original proposal did not adequately address [issue]. The revised Aim 2 (Sec 3.2) now includes [specific addition], with preliminary data shown in Fig. 4."

### Responding to a misunderstanding (politely)
> "We appreciate this comment and recognize that the prior wording was unclear. Our intended approach is [clarified statement]; we have rewritten Sec 3.1 (paragraph 2) to make this explicit."

### Adding a missing experiment / analysis
> "Following the reviewer's suggestion, we have added a [specific analysis] using [dataset/method]. The results, presented in Fig. 5 and discussed in Sec 4.3, [outcome]. This strengthens our [original claim]."

### Disagreeing politely (rare; use sparingly)
> "We respectfully note that [reviewer's premise] is one possible interpretation, but our results in [Section] indicate [counter-evidence]. We have nevertheless added a paragraph (Sec 5.2) discussing this alternative reading and bounding its plausibility."

### Bounding a limitation
> "The reviewer is correct that [limitation] applies to our approach. We have added a Limitations subsection (Sec 6) explicitly stating this and proposing it as future work."

### Closing
> "We thank the reviewers for their constructive critique. The resubmitted proposal is substantially strengthened in [3 specific dimensions], and we believe addresses the main concerns."

## Checklist Before Submitting Resubmission

- [ ] Every reviewer comment is addressed in the response document
- [ ] Every response cites a specific revised section / page
- [ ] Substantive revisions exceed cosmetic edits
- [ ] Tone is professional, not defensive
- [ ] Within length limits for the response document (NIH: 1 page Intro)
- [ ] No new contradictions introduced between sections
- [ ] Updated preliminary data, citations, team if relevant
- [ ] Cooling-off rules verified (ERC, sometimes Horizon)
