# Self-Grading Rubric

Use this rubric to score every draft before delivery. Score independently, then revise any criterion below threshold.

## Master Rubric (8 Criteria, 0–5 Scale, Total /40)

This rubric distills the Yale MB&B, UC Berkeley OFEW, NIH K99, NSF CAREER, and ERC StG criteria into a single instrument applicable to any research statement.

| # | Criterion | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Vision Clarity** | Is there a single, clearly-stated North Star sentence? Could a non-specialist paraphrase it? |
| 2 | **Problem Importance** | Is the problem's importance argued with specifics, not asserted with adjectives? |
| 3 | **Past-Work Evidence** | Does past work demonstrate the candidate can execute? Are claims quantified and impact concrete? |
| 4 | **Research Independence** | Does the candidate read as an independent researcher, not an advisor's extension? |
| 5 | **Future-Research Fundability** | Are future lines shaped to match grant criteria (NSF/NIH/ERC/NWO/UKRI)? Methods named, horizons stated, funding vectors identified? |
| 6 | **Departmental Fit** | Are 2–3 specific groups, 1–2 named faculty, 1 cross-cutting initiative, and 1 funding pathway tied to the target department? |
| 7 | **Voice & Mechanics** | First-person, active, low-hedging where appropriate; no anti-pattern phrases; correct length; clean prose. |
| 8 | **Authentic Motivation** (when brief asks for it) | Does motivation come from intellectual specificity rather than affect declarations? |

### 0–5 Scoring Scale (per criterion)

| Score | Descriptor | Diagnostic |
|---|---|---|
| **5** | Exceptional | Sets a benchmark; would impress a senior faculty reader |
| **4** | Strong | Clearly above adequate; minor refinements would help |
| **3** | Adequate | Meets baseline expectations; could go further |
| **2** | Weak | Identifiable problems; revision needed |
| **1** | Poor | Major problem; section likely to hurt the application |
| **0** | Missing | Not addressed or fundamentally absent |

If criterion 8 (Authentic Motivation) is not requested by the brief, **renormalize total to /35**.

### Total Score Interpretation

| Total /40 (or /35) | Interpretation | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 32+ / 28+ | Submit-ready | Final mechanics pass only |
| 24–31 / 21–27 | Submit-ready after revision | Address any criterion ≤2 |
| 16–23 / 14–20 | Substantial revision needed | Major rewrite of low-scoring sections |
| <16 / <14 | Restart | Document has structural problems beyond editing |

**Hard rule:** any criterion scoring 0 or 1 → revise that section before submission, regardless of total.

## Per-Criterion Scoring Anchors

### Criterion 1: Vision Clarity (0–5)

| Score | Anchor |
|---|---|
| 5 | One memorable sentence. Specific question or problem named. Falsifiable in spirit. Survives the "paraphrase test". |
| 4 | Vision present and clear, but not memorable; one extra sentence to reach the same content. |
| 3 | Vision discoverable in the opening but requires inference. |
| 2 | Vision is vague or trend-following ("I work in machine learning"). |
| 1 | No identifiable vision sentence. Opening reads as preamble. |
| 0 | No opening, or opening is autobiography/definition. |

**Paraphrase test:** read only paragraph 1, close the document, write the vision in your own words. If you cannot, score ≤2.

### Criterion 2: Problem Importance (0–5)

| Score | Anchor |
|---|---|
| 5 | Importance argued with concrete stakes, named consequences, or specific gaps. |
| 4 | Importance argued, but with one place where assertion replaces evidence. |
| 3 | Importance stated; supported but not vividly. |
| 2 | Importance asserted using "important", "critical", "fundamental" without grounding. |
| 1 | Importance assumed; reader expected to take it for granted. |
| 0 | No statement of importance. |

### Criterion 3: Past-Work Evidence (0–5)

| Score | Anchor |
|---|---|
| 5 | 2–3 themes; each PARI-shaped; impact quantified (citations, deployment, awards, scope). |
| 4 | Themes present; most have impact stated; 1–2 themes missing quantification. |
| 3 | Themes identifiable; impact mentioned; some chronology creeping in. |
| 2 | Past work organized chronologically; impact vague. |
| 1 | Past work reads as publications list or CV summary. |
| 0 | No coherent past-work section. |

### Criterion 4: Research Independence (0–5)

| Score | Anchor |
|---|---|
| 5 | Clear distinct research identity; advisor mentioned 0–1 times by name; future direction departs from dissertation. |
| 4 | Mostly independent; one or two phrases that could be tightened. |
| 3 | Independence present but partially obscured by collaboration framing. |
| 2 | Heavy "we" usage; "extending advisor's work"; identity unclear. |
| 1 | Reads as continuation of advisor/lab's program. |
| 0 | Statement could have been written by the advisor about themselves. |

### Criterion 5: Future-Research Fundability (0–5)

Each future-research line is checked against the 6-element shape (title, animating question, why-now, approach, what-it-enables, funding vector).

| Score | Anchor |
|---|---|
| 5 | 2–3 lines each with all 6 elements; risk balance (safer + ambitious); near-term projects named for years 1–2. |
| 4 | 2 lines with 5–6 elements each; risk balance present. |
| 3 | 2 lines with 4–5 elements; some lines underspecified. |
| 2 | 1–2 lines with 3 elements; methods or funding vectors missing. |
| 1 | Future research is hand-waving ("I plan to continue exploring..."). |
| 0 | No future-research section. |

### Criterion 6: Departmental Fit (0–5)

| Score | Anchor |
|---|---|
| 5 | Names 2–3 groups + 1–2 faculty (with a specific paper) + 1 cross-cutting initiative + 1 funding vector + a gap claim. |
| 4 | 4 of the 5 elements present. |
| 3 | 2–3 of the 5 elements present; somewhat specific. |
| 2 | Generic praise; one or two faculty names without specifics. |
| 1 | Pure generic praise ("world-class department"). |
| 0 | No fit paragraph. |

**Reuse test:** if you can paste the fit paragraph into a different application without major editing, score ≤2.

### Criterion 7: Voice & Mechanics (0–5)

| Score | Anchor |
|---|---|
| 5 | First-person consistent; tense discipline; no anti-pattern phrases (`red_flags.md`); within length cap; clean prose. |
| 4 | One or two minor lapses (e.g., one passive-voice sentence; one cliché). |
| 3 | Several mechanics issues but core voice is right. |
| 2 | Multiple anti-patterns or tense inconsistency. |
| 1 | Wrong register for region (US-hype in Dutch application, etc.). |
| 0 | Length significantly over cap, or major mechanics failures. |

### Criterion 8: Authentic Motivation (when brief requests it) (0–5)

| Score | Anchor |
|---|---|
| 5 | Motivation is shown via specific named questions and sustained engagement. Passes the ten-word test. |
| 4 | Motivation present and credible, with one or two affect-only sentences. |
| 3 | Motivation conveyed but with some cliché phrasing. |
| 2 | Motivation declared but not shown ("I am passionate about..."). |
| 1 | Motivation as boilerplate; childhood hook or generic affect. |
| 0 | No motivation paragraph despite brief asking for one. |

**Ten-word test:** strip affect words from each sentence. Does it still convey commitment? If no, score ≤2.

## Discipline-Specific Adjustments to Rubric

The criteria above apply to all disciplines, but **weighting varies**. See `discipline_variants.md` for per-discipline detail.

| Discipline | Highest-weighted criteria |
|---|---|
| CS / Engineering | 1 (vision), 5 (fundability), 6 (fit) |
| Life sciences | 3 (past-work evidence with preliminary data), 5 (NIH-shape), 6 (fit + lab resources) |
| Mathematics | 2 (problem importance — taste), 4 (independence), 5 (program of problems) |
| Social sciences | 1 (vision), 2 (theoretical contribution), 5 (data access) |
| Humanities | 3 (book project arc), 1 (theoretical framing), 6 (archives + library) |

## US-School Overlay Checks

When the target is a US institution, run these after the master rubric. They do not add a ninth criterion; instead, they cap the relevant score if missing.

| Missing US signal | Cap |
|---|---|
| Paragraph 1 does not state the long-term agenda / brand / North Star | Criterion 1 ≤3 |
| Main problem's "so what?" is not intelligible to a non-specialist department reader | Criterion 2 ≤3 |
| Past work does not identify the candidate's independent role in team/advisor work | Criterion 4 ≤3 |
| Future agenda lacks 2–3 concrete projects or a get-go / tenure-track / long-term horizon | Criterion 5 ≤3 |
| No funding, collaboration, facility, resource, or startup-feasibility signal where field expects it | Criterion 5 ≤3 and Criterion 6 ≤3 |
| Fit ignores institution type (R1 vs PUI vs teaching-research vs medical school) | Criterion 6 ≤3 |
| Headings, layout, jargon, acronyms, or paragraph length make skim-reading hard | Criterion 7 ≤3 |

## Calibration Sources

The rubric is calibrated against:

- **Yale MB&B Search Committee Rubric** (0–3 scale, 9 criteria) — life sciences benchmark.
- **UC Berkeley OFEW Faculty Candidate Rubric** (1–5 scale, 5 criteria) — generalist faculty benchmark.
- **NIH K99/R00 Review Criteria** (1–9 scale, 5 criteria) — biomedical career-development benchmark.
- **NSF CAREER Solicitation 20-525** — Intellectual Merit + Broader Impacts + Education Plan.
- **ERC StG Information for Applicants** — ground-breaking + ambition + feasibility + PI capacity.
- **US university career/writing-center guidance** — Cornell, CMU, MIT EECS, Caltech, Penn, Yale, Harvard, UConn, Notre Dame, Delaware, and Berkeley Math; see `us_school_guidance.md`.

Sources: Yale MB&B `https://mbb.yale.edu/sites/default/files/files/SrchCrit_ResPlan_8-15-22.pdf`; UC Berkeley OFEW `https://ofew.berkeley.edu/creating-rubric-assess-faculty-candidates`; NIH `https://grants.nih.gov/grants/peer/guidelines_general/scoring_system_and_procedure.pdf`; NSF CAREER `https://new.nsf.gov/funding/opportunities/faculty-early-career-development-program-career`; ERC StG `https://ec.europa.eu/info/funding-tenders/opportunities/docs/2021-2027/horizon/guidance/`.

## How to Use This Rubric When Reviewing Someone Else's Draft

1. Score each criterion independently. Do not let earlier scores influence later ones.
2. For any criterion ≤3, write a one-sentence diagnosis citing a specific line or paragraph.
3. Report scores in a table to the user. Do not summarize as "looks good".
4. Recommend the **single highest-leverage revision** first; subsequent revisions cascade from it.
5. Re-score after revision before declaring done.
