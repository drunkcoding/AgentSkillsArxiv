## 1. US vs NL Framing Differences

| Aspect | US "Teaching Statement" | NL / TU Delft "Education Statement" / Onderwijsvisie |
|---|---|---|
| Typical length | 1–2 pages | 1–3 pages (vacancy-specific) |
| Voice | Personal narrative dominant | Personal narrative + institutional grounding |
| Frameworks | Light, example-driven | Constructive Alignment, CDIO, BKO/UTQ explicitly known |
| Supervision weight | Often a paragraph | Explicit, central — a competence in its own right |
| Course mapping | "Courses I could teach" | Mapping to specific programmes + Q1–Q4 system |
| Future framing | Personal teaching trajectory | Vision aligned with institutional education vision |
| Use of theory | Cite sparingly | Cite institutional frameworks where genuinely apply |

**Practical implication**: a strong TU Delft statement reads as personal *and* institutionally fluent. It signals that you have read the TU Delft Vision on Education and know what UTQ entails — without name-dropping the framework as a substitute for substance.

## 2. The UTQ / BKO Competence Framework

**UTQ** (University Teaching Qualification) = **BKO** (Basis Kwalificatie Onderwijs) is the **mandatory teaching qualification** for new TU Delft faculty with >0.2 FTE teaching duties and <5 years experience. New hires must complete it within 3 years. It is recognised across all 4TU institutions (TU Delft, TU/e, U Twente, WUR) and broadly across Dutch research universities.

**Five competence domains** (4TU framework):

1. **Designing / Redesigning Education** — course design, intended learning outcomes, alignment of activities and assessment.
2. **Teaching and Supervising** — classroom delivery; supervising individual students and small groups (BSc/MSc theses, project teams).
3. **Assessment** — designing valid, reliable assessments; giving feedback; assessment of large cohorts.
4. **Evaluating Education** — collecting and acting on student feedback; iterative course improvement.
5. **Professionalisation / Educational Leadership** — staying current with educational research; contributing to programme/curriculum development.

**How to reference UTQ in your statement**:
- If certified or in progress: state it briefly as a fact.
- If not yet started: signal awareness ("I look forward to obtaining the UTQ within my first years…").
- **Do not** structure your entire statement as a UTQ portfolio. The statement is a vision; the UTQ portfolio is a separate dossier.

## 3. Constructive Alignment (Biggs)

**The principle**: intended learning outcomes (ILOs), teaching/learning activities, and assessment must all be aligned. If you ask students to *analyse and design*, your activities must let them *practise analysing and designing*, and your assessment must *assess analysis and design* — not just recall.

**Why it matters at TU Delft**: Constructive Alignment is the pedagogical backbone of the UTQ Fundamentals module. Most assessors will recognise the term; using it correctly signals fluency.

**Correct use in a statement**:
> "When I redesigned the recursion unit, I aligned three things: the ILO ('students can construct, trace, and reason about recursive procedures'), the activities (paired tracing exercises, live coding with deliberate base-case bugs, weekly Parsons problems), and the assessment (a code-construction exam where students annotate the call stack)."

**Misuse to avoid**: name-dropping "constructive alignment" without showing the alignment.

## 4. CDIO — TU Delft's Engineering Education Model

CDIO = **Conceive – Design – Implement – Operate**. TU Delft is a founding partner of the [CDIO Initiative](https://www.cdio.org/). The model frames engineering education around the full lifecycle of a real system, with active project-based learning at every level.

**CSE programme manifestation**:
- Year 1 has a Software Engineering Project (CSE1105).
- Year 2 has a 10-week Software Project (CSE2000) with an external client.
- Year 3 has a Research Project / Bachelor end-project.
- The model aligns with Project-Based Learning (PBL) and increasingly Challenge-Based Learning (CBL).

**Useful in your statement** if you have run client-coupled projects, integrated industry partners, or designed a multi-week implementation-oriented project. Avoid mentioning CDIO if you have no such experience — it reads as cargo-cult.

## 5. TU Delft Vision on Education 2024–2030 ("Durable Inspiration")

Key themes that align well with a strong CS education statement:

- **"Inspiring encounter between teachers and students"** — students and teachers meeting as intellectual partners; not transmission.
- **Challenge-Based Learning** — students tackle authentic, often interdisciplinary challenges with societal stakes.
- **Active learning** — flipped classrooms, in-class problem solving, peer instruction.
- **Two-way feedback** — students→teacher and teacher→students; iterative course improvement.
- **Critical engagement with AI** in education and research; both as subject matter and as a teaching tool.
- **Sustainability and SDG integration** — the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals as a recurring framing.
- **Interdisciplinarity** — increasingly between the BSc CSE and other engineering programmes.

**How to reference**: pick one or two themes that match your *actual* practice. "I have designed challenge-based assignments where students…" is far stronger than listing all the themes.

## 6. Quartile (Q) System & Implications

TU Delft runs **four 10-week quartiles** (Q1–Q4) per academic year, plus exam resit weeks. Most MSc courses are 5 EC and run within a single quartile.

**What this means for course design** (signal you understand it):
- Tighter time-on-task per week than a 14-week semester.
- Continuous assessment dominates over single end-of-term exams.
- Q-system constrains how much you can demand outside of class — students typically take 3 courses concurrently per quartile.
- Assignment scheduling must avoid clashes with the other concurrent Q-courses.

**Useful phrasing**: "The Q-system rewards concentrated, high-touch course design; in my experience, weekly small assessments with rapid feedback work better than mid-term + final under this rhythm."

## 7. T-Shaped Professional Model

TU Delft graduates as **T-shaped**: broad foundational knowledge across CS sub-disciplines (the horizontal bar of the T), with deep specialisation in their MSc theme (the vertical stem). This shapes:

- BSc CSE = breadth-first, solid foundations across programming, algorithms, systems, data, AI, security.
- MSc CS / DSAIT = depth-first within chosen themes.

**Implication for your statement**: when discussing BSc teaching, lean into breadth and foundational rigour. When discussing MSc, lean into depth, research-led teaching, and project-thesis supervision.

## 8. International / English-Medium Teaching

All three target programmes (BSc CSE, MSc CS, MSc DSAIT) are taught in **English**. Implications:

- Cohorts are highly international (often >50% non-Dutch).
- Cultural and educational-background heterogeneity is real and substantive — not a buzzword.
- English-as-a-second-language considerations are practical: clear slides, structured handouts, multimodal explanation.
- TU Delft does **not** require Dutch language for teaching, but Dutch acquisition is expected over time for academic-citizenship reasons.

**If applicable**: mention specific practices ("I provide all lectures with structured slide handouts and a glossary of CS-specific terms; international cohorts use these heavily as study scaffolds").

## 9. Ready-to-Adapt Phrasing

Use sparingly — typically one or two of these per statement, not all.

**Onderwijsvisie / vision opening**:
> "My vision of computer science education centres on [your principle], realised through [your method] and assessed through [your assessment]. This vision aligns with TU Delft's commitment to [specific element of TU Delft's education vision], and with the constructive alignment principle that anchors the UTQ."

**UTQ acknowledgement**:
> "I am [completing my UTQ / planning to obtain my UTQ within my first three years], and I view it as an opportunity to formalise practices I already use and to engage with TU Delft's educational community."

**CDIO / project-based fit**:
> "My experience designing semester-long client-coupled projects fits naturally with the CDIO-influenced project sequence in the BSc CSE — particularly the second-year Software Project and the third-year Research Project."

**Q-system awareness**:
> "I design my courses around the 10-week quartile rhythm: weekly low-stakes assessments with rapid feedback, two checkpoint deliverables, and a final integrative project, rather than a high-stakes single end-of-quartile exam."

**International cohort**:
> "Teaching internationally diverse cohorts is not a constraint but a resource: peer-learning across different prior curricula consistently exposes assumptions in 'standard' CS pedagogy that monocultural cohorts leave unexamined."

**Closing institutional fit**:
> "I would welcome contributing to the [Software Technology / Intelligent Systems] department's teaching mission across the BSc CSE, the MSc Computer Science, and the MSc Data Science and AI Technology — three programmes whose interlocking design is, in my view, one of TU Delft's distinctive educational strengths."
