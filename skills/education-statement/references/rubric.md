## 1. The CRLT Rubric (Kaplan, Meizlish, O'Neal & Wright, 2008)

This is the most widely adopted rubric in the US, and structurally aligns with how Dutch UTQ assessors evaluate the *Designing Education* + *Teaching & Supervising* + *Assessment* + *Evaluation* competencies.

| Category | Excellent | Adequate | Unsatisfactory |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Goals for student learning** | Specific; go beyond knowledge to skills, attitudes, dispositions; concise; tied to discipline | Vague or generic; mostly knowledge-level | Missing or superficial |
| **Enactment of goals (methods)** | Specific examples within disciplinary context; methods clearly connected to goals | Generic methods, no examples | Methods absent or disconnected from goals |
| **Assessment of goals** | Specific assessment tools aligned to goals; explains how assessments communicate disciplinary priorities | Mentions assessment without explanation | Assessment absent |
| **Inclusive learning environment** | Coherent inclusion philosophy integrated throughout; addresses one or more of: structural inequality awareness, diverse approaches, belonging | Mentions inclusion as a list item, disconnected from practice | Not addressed or addressed awkwardly |
| **Structure, rhetoric, language** | Guiding theme; jargon avoided; teaching terms given discipline-specific definitions; correct grammar | Some jargon; structure present but not connected to ideas | No structure; heavy jargon; disconnected claims |

**Use this as a final pass.** For each row, ask: *which row would a fair-minded reviewer place me in?*

## 2. Search-Committee Chair Priorities

Meizlish & Kaplan's survey of 164 search committee chairs ranked these as the most-cited criteria:

1. **Evidence of practice** — *most-cited*. Specific examples of how you enact your philosophy. (110 of 164 mentions.)
2. **Reflectiveness** — describing how you have changed your teaching, what you have learned, what you still want to develop. (53 mentions.)
3. **Conveys valuing of teaching** — the statement should sound like you take this part of the job seriously, not like an afterthought.
4. **Well written, clear, readable** — basic prose quality.
5. **Student-centred** — focus on what students *do*, not only what you do.

**Implication for drafting**: when in doubt, replace another claim about your beliefs with another specific example of practice.

## 3. Section-by-Section Self-Critique

For each section, score 1–3 (1 = needs major revision, 2 = adequate, 3 = strong).

### Opening
- Does the first sentence sound like *only you* could have written it? (Generic openers fail here.)
- Does the opening foreshadow the philosophy that follows, or is it disconnected?
- Could the opening be cut without losing anything? If yes, rewrite.

### Philosophy / Vision
- Are there 2–4 named principles? (More than 4 dilutes; fewer than 2 thin.)
- Is each principle in *your words*, not jargon-borrowed?
- Are the principles distinguishable? (Two principles that say the same thing twice are one principle.)
- Does each principle have a discipline-specific definition, not just a generic one?

### Methods in Action
- Does each principle from §2 reappear here with a specific in-class activity?
- Is there at least one numeric outcome, student quote pattern, or before/after comparison?
- Could a non-CS reviewer follow each example?
- Are there examples from different course types (intro / advanced / project / lab)?

### Supervision
- Does it explicitly address 1:1 work *and* small groups?
- Does it differentiate BSc thesis vs MSc thesis vs PhD supervision style?
- Is there at least one named student outcome (anonymised), e.g., "one BSc thesis I supervised in 2024 became a workshop publication at…"?
- Is the supervision philosophy distinguishable from the classroom philosophy, or are they collapsed?

### Courses
- Are at least 3 specific course names from the target programmes mentioned?
- Are they verified from the current study guide (not invented or outdated)?
- Is at least one *new* course proposed, with a one-sentence rationale?
- Is the language modal ("I would teach…") not commanding ("I will teach…")?

### Closing
- Does the closing add something new (forward vision, growth, contribution promise) rather than restate the opening?
- Does it tie to the specific institution?
- Is it under 120 words?

### Whole-document
- Length under 1.5 pages (or under cap)?
- One coherent voice from start to finish?
- No unsupported claim of importance, novelty, or excellence?
- No phrases from the cliche list (see structure_and_pitfalls.md §8)?

## 4. Mock-Reviewer Questions

Read the statement and ask each of these. If you cannot answer **yes**, revise.

1. *Could this statement be signed by another candidate in my subfield?* If yes, it is too generic.
2. *What is one in-class activity this candidate uses that I can vividly picture?* If you cannot picture even one, the statement is too abstract.
3. *What is the candidate's stance on a contested question — assessment style, LLM use, group work, lecturing vs. flipped?* If the statement avoids every contested choice, it lacks identity.
4. *How does this candidate's research connect to their teaching?* If the answer is "vaguely, via a hand-wave," strengthen the link.
5. *Would I want this person teaching my students at our institution?* This is the actual decision the committee makes; the statement should make it easy to say yes.
6. *If a student in this candidate's class is struggling at week 6, what specifically does the candidate do?* The answer is the supervision/feedback/inclusion test.
7. *What does this candidate uniquely bring that we do not already have?* This is the differentiation test.

## 5. Red Flags Committees See Most Often

Ranked roughly by frequency in committee debriefs and teaching-centre guides:

1. **All philosophy, no practice.** A page of beliefs with zero classroom detail.
2. **Course-list as statement.** Bullet points of "Courses I can teach" with no philosophy or examples.
3. **Theory parade.** Five educational theorists cited, none applied.
4. **Saccharine generalities.** "Students are the future. I love sharing knowledge." — could be auto-generated.
5. **Length violation.** Three pages where one was requested. Reads as a candidate who cannot follow instructions.
6. **Ignores supervision.** Completely absent in a EU/NL application — automatic weakness signal.
7. **Inflated claims.** "I revolutionised the curriculum at X" — usually unsupported.
8. **Misalignment with the department.** Statement describes a teaching style obviously incompatible with the programme (e.g., heavy seminar-only style for a project-based engineering programme).
9. **Untreated weaknesses.** Thin teaching record presented as if it were strong; reflective candidates instead acknowledge and describe how they have prepared.
10. **Mechanical errors.** Typos, mixed tenses, wrong institution name pasted from a previous application. The fastest way to get rejected.

## Final Pass (5 minutes)

1. Print or read on a different screen.
2. Cover the bottom half; does the top half hook a busy reader?
3. Cover the top half; does the bottom half land a clear forward vision?
4. Search-and-destroy: "passionate", "diverse learners", "real world", "open door", "critical thinking" (more than once).
5. Verify every named course exists in the current curriculum.
6. Confirm institution name, programme names, and applicant's own name are correct.
