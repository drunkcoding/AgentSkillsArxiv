# Future Research Section

This is the section that gets the candidate the interview. It must look like the embryo of a fundable proposal — reviewers mentally cost it.

## The 6-Element Fundable-Line Shape

Each future-research line must contain all six elements. If any is missing, the line reads as wishful thinking, not a research program.

| # | Element | Example wording |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Title** (4–8 words, paper-title shape) | *"Composable Verification for ML Systems"* — not *"Future Plans on AI"* |
| 2 | **Animating question** (1 sentence) | *"How do we verify systems whose components are learned from data rather than specified by humans?"* |
| 3 | **Why now** (1 sentence) | *"Recent advances in [tool/technique] make [previously hard] tractable."* |
| 4 | **Approach** (1–2 sentences) | Named methods, not platitudes. *"I will adapt session-type theory to ML pipelines, building on my prior work on [X]."* |
| 5 | **What it enables** (1 sentence) | Downstream impact, who benefits. *"The result is verification tooling that ML engineers can use without proof-assistant expertise."* |
| 6 | **Funding/collaboration vector** (half a sentence) | *"This is a natural fit for an NSF CAREER / NWO Vidi / ERC StG."* |

## How Many Lines?

| Length cap | Number of lines | Profile |
|---|---|---|
| 1 page | 1 (the central one) | High specificity, tight |
| 2 pages | 2 lines | One safer, one ambitious |
| 3 pages | 2–3 lines | Add high-risk if 3 |
| 4–5 pages | 3 lines | Safer + ambitious + high-risk |
| Humanities | The "next book" + 1 article-cluster line | Different shape |

**Default: 2 mandatory lines** (one rich-but-safer, one ambitious-but-defensible) **+ 1 optional high-risk line**. More than 3 dilutes; fewer than 2 looks one-trick.

## Risk Calibration

| Line type | Purpose | Length |
|---|---|---|
| **Safer line** | Demonstrates you can deliver in years 1–2; uses existing methods extended in new ways | Detailed (≈200 words) |
| **Ambitious line** | Shows where the field could go; harder problem, plausible path | Detailed (≈200 words) |
| **High-risk line** (optional) | One paragraph "and beyond"; signals taste and ambition without commitment | Short (≈80–120 words) |

A reviewer should think: *"They could fail on the high-risk line and still have a successful career."* If the whole agenda depends on the most ambitious line, the agenda is fragile.

## Horizon Planning

Each line should contain at least one **near-term project** (years 1–2) — what the first PhD student in the candidate's group would work on. NWO/Veni and NSF reviewers explicitly look for this.

> *"In the first two years, I will pursue [specific sub-problem] using [existing toolkit], producing [specific output]. This sets up the longer-term goal of [bigger question]."*

This is the "fundable shape" signal. Lines without near-term projects look like research dreams; lines with them look like research programs.

### US Multi-Horizon Overlay

US school guidance uses multiple time windows at once. UConn emphasizes 1–3 year goals; Cornell / Yale / Notre Dame often frame 3–5 years; MIT / Harvard / Caltech expect a 5–10 year vision in many STEM searches. For US statements, each future section should therefore expose three horizons:

| Horizon | What the reader needs to see |
|---|---|
| **Get-go** | A project a first student, first postdoc, or the candidate can start immediately with available methods/resources. |
| **Tenure-track** | 2–3 concrete thrusts for years 1–6, with methods, outputs, collaborators, and likely funding. |
| **Long-term** | A 5–10 year field-level destination that shows scientific growth beyond the dissertation/postdoc lab. |

Do not present these as a rigid year-by-year schedule unless the brief asks for a 5-year plan. Instead, make sure the future section contains all three signals.

## Connecting Future to Past

Every future-research line should contain at least one sentence connecting it to past work as **evidence of feasibility**:

> *"My prior work on [X] established the [methodological building block] this line requires."*

The reader's silent question is always: *"Can they actually do this?"* The connection sentence answers it without bragging.

## Funding Vocabulary by Region

Use the right vocabulary for the target region. Wrong vocabulary signals the candidate hasn't done their homework.

| Region | Funding bodies / instruments to name |
|---|---|
| US | NSF (CAREER, core/standard), NIH (K99/R00, R01, R21, K-series), DOE, DARPA/DoD, NASA, USDA, NEH/ACLS/Mellon (humanities), Sloan/Moore/Simons/ACS PRF/foundations, industrial partners |
| EU (multinational) | ERC (Starting / Consolidator), Horizon Europe, Marie Skłodowska-Curie |
| Netherlands | NWO Veni / Vidi / Vici, KNAW, NWO Open Technology Programme, Sectorplan |
| UK | UKRI (EPSRC, BBSRC, ESRC, AHRC), Royal Society (URF), Leverhulme |
| Germany | DFG (Emmy Noether, Heisenberg), Helmholtz, BMBF |
| Switzerland | SNSF (Eccellenza, Starting Grant, Sinergia) |
| France | ANR, CNRS |
| Sweden | VR (Swedish Research Council), Wallenberg Foundations |
| Canada | NSERC (Discovery, CRC), CIHR, SSHRC |
| Australia | ARC (DECRA, DP, FT), NHMRC |

Naming **2 funding instruments** by their actual names + indicating which line maps to which is the right amount. More looks try-hard. None looks naïve.

## Industrial Collaboration

Where appropriate (CS, engineering, applied life sciences), naming a **specific industrial collaboration vector** strengthens the future-research section:

> *"This line has natural alignment with [Company]'s work on [X]; I have prior contact with [team / researcher]."*

Caveats:
- Only name companies you have a real prospect of collaborating with. Do not invent or speculate.
- Industrial collaboration must complement, not replace, public funding plans.
- For Dutch / EU positions, industrial collaboration counts heavily toward "valorization" criteria.

## What Makes a Future-Research Section Fail

In order of frequency:

1. **Vague titles** — "More work on AI" instead of named research questions.
2. **No near-term projects** — only 5-year-out promises.
3. **No methods named** — lots of "I will study" without "using".
4. **Dependence on a single paper** — "I will extend [my paper]" instead of opening a research program.
5. **No funding vector** — looks like the candidate doesn't know how to support a group.
6. **Five+ shallow lines** instead of 2–3 deep lines.
7. **Future lines untethered from past work** — a sudden pivot to an unrelated area without justification.
8. **Future lines untethered from each other** — no through-line; reads as a project list.
