# Past-Work Section

Past work serves one purpose: to be **evidence** for the vision stated in the opening. It is not autobiography, not a publications list, and not a chronological narrative.

## The PARI Micro-Structure

Every past-work paragraph follows Problem → Approach → Result → Impact:

| Element | Length | Content |
|---|---|---|
| **Problem** | 1 sentence | The gap or question that mattered. Why anyone should care. |
| **Approach** | 1–2 sentences | The technical move you made. Name the technique. |
| **Result** | 1 sentence | What you proved / built / measured. **Quantify if possible.** |
| **Impact** | 1 sentence | Who used it. What venue published it. What it enabled. |

A reviewer should be able to point at any past-work paragraph and answer in one sentence: *what is the contribution?* If they cannot, the paragraph is fluff.

## Theme Selection (Critical)

The single biggest mistake is **organizing past work chronologically**. The single biggest improvement is **organizing by theme**.

A theme is an intellectual through-line that:

1. Connects 2+ projects.
2. Supports your vision sentence.
3. Could survive without any individual paper.

For 2–3 page statements: **2–3 themes**. For 4–5 page: 3 themes maximum. More than 3 themes signals "I lack a vision" — every reviewer's worst diagnosis.

### Theme Selection Worksheet

For each theme candidate, answer:

1. What is the **one-sentence claim** of this theme? (e.g., "I have shown that compositional verification scales to industrial-strength systems.")
2. Which **2–4 papers** support this claim?
3. How does this theme **serve the vision** in the opening?
4. Could this theme **survive** the deletion of any one paper? (If no, the theme is too narrow.)

If a paper does not fit any theme, **omit it**. The CV lists everything; the research statement is curated.

## Quantification Guidance

The Result and Impact sentences should always be specific. Quantify when possible:

| Vague | Specific |
|---|---|
| "Significant speedup" | "2.3× speedup over the prior best system" |
| "Many citations" | "Over 200 citations in 3 years" |
| "Adopted in industry" | "Deployed at [Company]; runs on [scale]" |
| "Published widely" | "Three papers at [top venue], one at [other venue]" |
| "Won awards" | "Best Paper at [venue, year]" |
| "Improved performance" | "Reduced error rate from 12% to 3.4% on [benchmark]" |

If you don't have hard numbers, use **scope** ("six benchmarks", "four industrial partners") or **adoption** ("now used by the X team").

## Advisor / Co-Author Independence Rules

A research statement must signal that **you can lead a research group**. This requires careful framing of jointly-authored work.

| ❌ Avoid | ✅ Use Instead |
|---|---|
| "Working under Prof. X..." | "In collaboration with Prof. X..." (or no mention) |
| "Extending my advisor's work on..." | "I built on the X line of work by..." (or claim it) |
| "We developed..." (when it was your idea) | "I developed... (with collaborators)" |
| "My advisor and I proved..." | "I proved..." (cite jointly only if needed) |
| Plural "we" throughout | First-person "I" for your contributions |

The rule: **claim what you led, attribute what you contributed to, omit what was incidental**. Do not pad past work with co-authorship lines that do not signal independence.

If a paper is genuinely 50/50 with another author, name them once, then describe the contribution as a project. Do not repeat the co-author throughout.

## Connecting Themes Back to Vision

After describing each theme, add a half-sentence connector to the vision:

> "These results address one half of my broader vision: \<connector\>."

This forces the reader to keep the vision in mind. Without these connectors, past work reads as a portfolio review rather than evidence.

## What to Cut

If past work is over-length, cut in this order:

1. **Side projects** that don't serve any theme.
2. **Methodology details** that belong in the cited paper, not the statement.
3. **Co-author attribution** beyond the first mention.
4. **Hedging language** ("we hope this might suggest...") — assert results.
5. **Background sentences** that explain a subfield to a reviewer who already knows it.

Never cut: the result, the impact, the connector to vision.

## Allocation by Length

| Length cap | Past-work share | # Themes | Words per theme |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 page | ~30% (≈150 words) | 1–2 themes, very compressed | ~80 words each |
| 2 pages | ~35% (≈350 words) | 2–3 themes | ~120 words each |
| 3 pages | ~35–40% (≈500 words) | 2–3 themes | ~170 words each |
| 4–5 pages | ~30% (≈600 words) | 3 themes | ~200 words each |
| Humanities 7–10 pages | ~25% (book project arc) | Often a single dissertation-to-book theme | See `discipline_variants.md` |
