# Red-Flag Phrases and Patterns

This is the active blacklist. Every flagged item must be removed or replaced before submission. Catalogued from Andy Pavlo (CMU), Karen Kelsky (The Professor Is In), Adrian Sampson (Cornell), GradPilot, Cynthia Lee (Stanford), Valdosta State "Kisses of Death", and direct review of bad-statement examples.

## How to Use This Reference

When reviewing a draft, search for **literal phrases** in the table below. Each match → revise using the "Replacement" column. Several anti-patterns are also patterns (passive voice, list addiction) — scan for those structurally.

## Top 20 Red Flags with Replacements

### 1. The Childhood Origin Hook
- **Phrases:** "Ever since I was young...", "When I was [age], my father gave me a computer...", "From an early age, I have been fascinated by..."
- **Why it fails:** Andy Pavlo: "Nobody believes you. Nobody wants to work with somebody like that." Reaching back to childhood signals you don't have anything more recent or substantive.
- **Frequency:** "Ever since I was young" appears ~1,779×/year in US PhD applications.
- **Replacement:** Open with the current research interest or a specific intellectual encounter from undergraduate or PhD work.

### 2. The Passion Declaration
- **Phrases:** "I am passionate about...", "I have always been passionate about...", "I am fascinated by...", "I love..."
- **Why it fails:** ~1,451× appearances/year. Declared without evidence = noise. Indistinguishable from boilerplate.
- **Replacement:** Show passion through specifics. *"I have spent six years on a single question: \<specific question\>."* See `personal_voice.md` for full phrasebook.

### 3. The World-Changer
- **Phrases:** "I want to make a difference", "This research will revolutionize...", "My work will transform...", "groundbreaking", "paradigm-shifting"
- **Why it fails:** ~1,370× appearances. Generic altruism reads as naivety. Karen Kelsky: "Everybody wants to do good. We know this already."
- **Replacement:** Be specific about stakes. *"My near-term goal is to develop a method for X that addresses Y in Z context."*

### 4. The "No One Has Studied This" Gap
- **Phrases:** "However, no one to date has discussed...", "There is a gap in the literature on..."
- **Why it fails:** Karen Kelsky's "Why Are There No Elephants?" — readers ask "why should we care?" Unstudied ≠ important.
- **Replacement:** Educate the reader about why the gap matters. *"Scholars have not attended to X; meanwhile, recent advances in Y create a new opportunity to study Z."*

### 5. The Pseudo-Theoretical Hedge
- **Phrases:** "I conclude that gender plays a significant role in...", "Policy is not linear or static, but multidimensional...", "I argue that this is an example of neoliberalism"
- **Why it fails:** Karen Kelsky: "The job search is not a striptease." Non-statements that withhold the real claim. Reviewers get bored.
- **Replacement:** State the actual claim. *"Gender shapes X through Y mechanism, as my Z case study demonstrates."*

### 6. Statements Anyone Could Make
- **Phrases:** "I can teach a variety of courses in the department", "Your department offers many opportunities for collaboration", "Computer science is a rapidly evolving field"
- **Why it fails:** If anyone could write it, it doesn't help you. Wastes space on the most expensive section.
- **Replacement:** Replace with specifics. Name courses, name faculty, name a specific recent development.

### 7. The Open-Ended Researcher
- **Phrases:** "I am open to any area of research", "I love all of [field]", "I plan to pursue interesting questions as they arise"
- **Why it fails:** Signals lack of focus. Committees want directional researchers.
- **Replacement:** Name 2–3 concrete research questions for years 1–5.

### 8. Studying "Under" an Advisor
- **Phrases:** "I studied under Professor X", "Working under my advisor...", "My work builds on my advisor's research"
- **Why it fails:** Karen Kelsky's rule. Signals you are a student, not a peer. Reads as deferential.
- **Replacement:** *"I collaborated with Prof. X on Y project, where I contributed Z."* Or simply describe your work without hierarchy.

### 9. The I-Statement Avalanche
- **Phrases:** "I did X. I discovered Y. I believe Z. I am interested in W."
- **Why it fails:** Kelsky's Golden Rule violated: "Talk About the Research, and Not About Yourself." Buries the research under ego.
- **Replacement:** Start at least half of sentences with the research, the question, or the result. *"The study revealed X" instead of "I found that X."*

### 10. Generic Department Flattery
- **Phrases:** "Your world-class faculty and state-of-the-art facilities", "I am honored to apply to your prestigious department", "X University is renowned in [field]"
- **Why it fails:** Empty if you can't follow with specifics. WriteIvy: "You aren't in a position of authority to judge its resources."
- **Replacement:** Name 1–2 faculty whose work aligns, with a specific paper or project. See `department_fit.md`.

### 11. Passive Voice Obscuring Your Role
- **Phrases:** "Experiments were conducted", "Results were analyzed", "The study was designed"
- **Why it fails:** Adrian Sampson (Cornell): obscures your contribution. The reader can't tell what you specifically did.
- **Replacement:** Active voice with explicit role. *"I designed the experimental protocol, conducted all experiments, and analyzed results using X."*

### 12. The Reader-Condescension
- **Phrases:** "It goes without saying that...", "As everyone knows,...", "Obviously, X"
- **Why it fails:** Either condescending to experts or signaling you can't be bothered to argue.
- **Replacement:** State the assumption briefly and move on.

### 13. The Generic Lesson Learned
- **Phrases:** "This project taught me the importance of collaboration", "I learned the value of clear communication", "This experience showed me how research works"
- **Why it fails:** Adrian Sampson: "These are exactly the things that everybody learns when giving their first talk." Says nothing distinctive.
- **Replacement:** Replace with a technical contribution. *"I implemented a distributed consensus module that reduced recovery time from 200ms to 40ms."*

### 14. Forward-Looking Pleasantries (closing)
- **Phrases:** "I look forward to...", "I would be honored to...", "Thank you for considering my application"
- **Why it fails:** This is cover-letter content, not research-statement content. Wastes space.
- **Replacement:** Close with the research credo. See `personal_voice.md` Pattern G.

### 15. The List Addiction
- **Phrases:** "My research spans many areas, including X, Y, Z, W, V, U..."
- **Why it fails:** Karen Kelsky: lists multiply because the candidate has not chosen a focus. Reads as undifferentiated.
- **Replacement:** Name 2–3 themes. Drop the rest. The CV lists everything.

### 16. The Begging
- **Phrases:** "I believe I would be an asset to your department", "I hope you will consider...", "I am well prepared to meet these goals"
- **Why it fails:** Karen Kelsky: "Desperate and cloying." Defensive, not assertive.
- **Replacement:** Assert with evidence. *"My work on X positions me to contribute Y to your department's existing strength in Z."*

### 17. The Apologetic Ambition
- **Phrases:** "I know this might sound ambitious, but...", "Although it may seem far-fetched...", "I realize this is a tall order..."
- **Why it fails:** Apologizing weakens the claim. Dutch directness rule especially: state ambitions plainly.
- **Replacement:** State the ambition. Then substantiate with a feasibility argument.

### 18. The Trauma Disclosure
- **Phrases:** "When my [family member] got [illness], I realized...", "Coming from a difficult background, I..."
- **Why it fails:** Inappropriate for research statements unless directly tied to intellectual trajectory. Reads as exploitative.
- **Replacement:** Save for diversity statement (US R1) or omit. The research statement is about the research.

### 19. Trend-Surfing Openings
- **Phrases:** "In recent years, [hot topic] has...", "With the rise of LLMs/blockchain/AI/quantum...", "In today's digital age,..."
- **Why it fails:** Ages instantly. Signals trend-following rather than research-direction-setting.
- **Replacement:** Open with a specific gap or vision, not a trend.

### 20. The Quantification-Free Result
- **Phrases:** "Significant improvement", "Many citations", "Wide adoption", "Highly cited"
- **Why it fails:** Reads as vague. Reviewers calibrate "significant" against a hidden threshold.
- **Replacement:** Specific numbers. *"2.3× speedup"; "200+ citations"; "deployed at 4 industrial partners"; "Best Paper Award at \<venue\>".*

## Patterns (Not Single Phrases) to Scan For

These are structural anti-patterns. Scan the whole draft.

### Pattern A: Chronological Past Work
- Past-work paragraphs ordered by year of publication or year of project. Should be **theme-ordered**.
- Diagnostic: do paragraphs begin with "In 2019, I...", "Then in 2021, I..."? If yes, restructure.

### Pattern B: Future Research as a Single Continuation
- Only one future direction, framed as "I will continue to extend [my dissertation]."
- Diagnostic: future research must open at least one new line beyond the dissertation/postdoc.

### Pattern C: Fit Paragraph as Praise
- Fit paragraph contains adjectives ("excellent", "world-class", "innovative") about the department but no concrete intellectual hook.
- Diagnostic: count specific names of people, papers, projects, initiatives. If <4, revise.

### Pattern D: Methodology Hidden in the Past
- Methods are described in past-work paragraphs but not in future-research lines.
- Diagnostic: pick one future line. Can you name the methods? If no, revise.

### Pattern E: Vision Drift
- The opening states one vision; future-research section commits to a different vision.
- Diagnostic: highlight the vision sentence. Confirm every future line maps to it.

### Pattern F: Length Padding
- Sentences contain adverbs and qualifiers that add no information.
- Diagnostic: a draft that hits the cap with 2 hours of work likely needs 3 hours of cutting.

### Pattern G: The Disconnected Closing
- Closing paragraph reintroduces a topic not in the rest of the document, or pivots to the cover letter's content.
- Diagnostic: should be a credo or compressed restatement of vision. See `personal_voice.md` Pattern G.

## Pre-Submission Anti-Pattern Scan (5 minutes)

Before submitting, run this scan:

1. Search the document for: "passion", "passionate", "love", "fascinated", "novel", "groundbreaking", "revolutionary", "always been", "ever since", "world-class", "honored to" → flag every instance.
2. Count first-person verbs starting paragraphs ("I did", "I worked", "I developed"). If >50% of paragraphs, revise voice balance.
3. Count adjectives in fit paragraph ("excellent", "renowned", "leading"). If >2, revise.
4. Read the opening sentence aloud. Does it state a problem, a gap, or a vision? If no, revise.
5. Read the closing sentence aloud. Is it a credo or a hope? If a hope, revise.
