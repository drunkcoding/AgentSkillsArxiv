# Supervision Section — Drafting Guide

> **Purpose**: How to write the supervision paragraph(s) that the TU Delft prompt explicitly requests. Supervision is a discrete competence at TU Delft (an explicit UTQ domain) — not a footnote to classroom teaching.

## 1. Why Supervision Deserves Its Own Section

The TU Delft prompt explicitly asks: *"your perspective on teaching and supervision, both in classroom settings and when working with individual students or small groups."*

In Dutch academia:
- BSc end project, MSc thesis, and PhD supervision are major workload items.
- The UTQ has *Teaching and Supervising* as a single competence — the institution treats them as paired skills.
- PhD students are **employees** in the Netherlands, not students paying tuition. The legal and cultural relationship differs from the US.

Statements that skip supervision read as *missing* — the committee will infer either inexperience or insufficient attention to the prompt.

## 2. What to Cover (4-element template)

Aim for **100–180 words / 1 paragraph** (or two short paragraphs). Cover:

1. **Your supervision philosophy.** One named stance: e.g., "scaffolding-and-fading toward intellectual independence", "co-design of the project with the student", "weekly written feedback culture".
2. **Structural practices.** Concrete logistics: meeting frequency, format (1:1 vs lab meeting), feedback mechanism (written, on draft, in code review).
3. **Differentiation by level.** A sentence on how BSc vs MSc vs PhD supervision differs in your hands.
4. **One specific outcome.** A named (anonymised) example of a student's trajectory under your supervision.

## 3. Differentiating BSc / MSc / PhD Supervision

Strong statements show that supervision style scales with student maturity. Common shape:

| Level | Default style | Risk to manage |
|---|---|---|
| **BSc end project (10 EC, ~10 weeks)** | More **scaffolding**: explicit milestones, weekly check-ins, structured deliverables. Project scope tightly bounded. | Under-scoping (student finishes early with shallow result) or over-scoping (student misses deadline). |
| **MSc thesis (~30 EC, 6–9 months)** | **Co-design** of research question with student; transition from scaffolded to autonomous over the project. Bi-weekly meetings; written intermediate reports. | Student isolation in long-form research; mid-project drift. |
| **PhD (4 years, employee)** | **Apprenticeship → autonomy**: dense first-year mentorship, transitioning to independent research direction by year 3. | First-paper paralysis; supervisor over-direction stunting independence. |

**Useful framing**: "I scaffold heavily early, then deliberately fade my involvement so the student takes ownership before the final third of the project."

## 4. Concrete Supervision Practices to Name

Pick 1–3 practices you actually do:

- **Weekly written agenda** sent by the student before each meeting (forces them to articulate progress and blockers).
- **Shared project log / lab notebook** the student maintains; you read asynchronously between meetings.
- **Written feedback on drafts within 1 week** as a standing commitment.
- **Code review as supervision tool** — pull-request style review of student code, not just thesis chapters.
- **Group "lab meetings"** weekly or biweekly, where each student presents 5–10 minutes; peers give feedback before you do.
- **Mid-project written checkpoint** — a 2-page document the student writes summarising where the research stands and what remains; surfaces drift early.
- **Public-research artefacts** — student supervised toward a workshop/conference paper, an open-source release, a public dataset, or a reproducible artefact.
- **End-of-project reflection** — student writes a 1-page reflection on what they learned about *doing research*, not just the technical result.

These are credible because they are operational, not aspirational.

## 5. Frameworks Worth Knowing (Use Sparingly)

You can name **at most one** framework. Pick the one that genuinely shapes your practice.

- **Cognitive apprenticeship** (Lave & Wenger / Collins, Brown & Holum): novice supervised in legitimate peripheral participation, gradually moving to the centre. Excellent for PhD supervision framing.
- **Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)** + **scaffolding & fading**: tasks calibrated just beyond what the student can do alone, with support that decreases as they grow. Excellent for BSc/MSc supervision.
- **Vitae Researcher Development Framework**: UK/EU framework with four domains (Knowledge & Skills, Personal Effectiveness, Research Governance, Engagement & Influence). Useful when you want to signal a structured developmental view of researcher growth.
- **Gurr's "Rackety Bridge" / Gatfield's supervisory styles grid**: matrix of supervisor structure × student autonomy; explicitly recommends adapting style as the student gains competence.

**Do not** stack multiple frameworks. Pick one, apply it concretely.

## 6. Sample Paragraphs (CS-specific)

### Sample 1 — ML/Systems candidate, scaffolding-and-fading framing

> Supervision, for me, is the deliberate fading of scaffolds. With BSc end-project students I begin with a tightly-scoped problem, weekly checkpoints, and a shared issue tracker; by week 6 I expect them to set the next two weeks' agenda themselves. With MSc thesis students I co-design the research question and meet bi-weekly, but the cadence of written checkpoints rises in the middle third — that is where most theses drift, and a 2-page mid-project memo surfaces drift before it costs a month. With PhD students, my role in year one is to teach the craft of research; by year three I expect them to disagree with me confidently and on substance. A recent MSc thesis I supervised on [topic] became a workshop paper at [venue] — but the result I am proudest of is that the student now runs their own MSc supervisees in industry research using a similar structure.

### Sample 2 — HCI/Software Engineering candidate, lab-meeting framing

> Beyond 1:1 meetings, I run a weekly group meeting that I treat as the central pedagogical structure of my lab. Each student presents 5 minutes of "what I tried, what I learned, what I am stuck on"; peers respond before I do. Two effects compound. First, students develop the muscle of giving and receiving rigorous feedback — a skill that lecture courses do not teach. Second, the group becomes a shared cognitive resource: students learn from each other's failures faster than from my advice alone. For BSc end-project students this is especially valuable: a single 10-week project would otherwise feel isolating, but a recurring audience of peer reviewers turns it into apprenticeship in a community.

### Sample 3 — Theoretical CS candidate, written-feedback framing

> My supervision philosophy centres on written feedback. Every draft a student sends — a proof sketch, a thesis chapter, an experimental result — I return within a week with margin comments. The benefit is not the comments themselves but the artefact of revision: students see their own thinking change across versions, and a thesis becomes a record of their growth as a researcher rather than a single deliverable. For MSc theses I require a 2-page mid-project memo and a 1-page end-project reflection; for BSc end projects I scope to a single research question with a clear go/no-go decision at week 5. The clearest signal of my approach is an MSc thesis (2024) where the student's first draft and final draft, side by side, show a researcher learning to argue rather than report.

## What Not to Do

- Generic supervision claims ("I am a supportive mentor").
- Treating supervision as a CV bullet ("I have supervised 12 students").
- Skipping the BSc level — TU Delft has heavy BSc end-project supervision load and committees notice.
- Promising a hands-off philosophy that contradicts the BSc reality (BSc students need scaffolding).
- Saying nothing about how 1:1 supervision differs from your classroom teaching.
