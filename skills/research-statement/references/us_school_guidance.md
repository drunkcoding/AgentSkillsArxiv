# US School Guidance Overlay

Use this reference for **any US faculty or postdoc research statement** and whenever the user cites advice from Cornell, CMU, MIT, Caltech, Penn, Yale, Harvard, UConn, Notre Dame, Delaware, Berkeley, Stanford, Purdue, or similar US university career/writing centers.

The US guidance corpus converges on one message: the statement is a **research-only, committee-readable, forward-looking argument** that the candidate has a coherent identity, can run an independent program, can attract resources, and will fit this department's students, facilities, collaborators, and institutional mission.

## Source-Calibrated Rules

### 1. Reader Model: Busy, Mixed-Expertise Faculty

Cornell, CMU, MIT, Yale, Caltech, and Berkeley all assume a search committee with members outside the candidate's subfield. Write so a field-literate non-specialist can follow the first page, while specialists still see technical substance later.

**Operational test:** pick two imagined readers: one close to the candidate's subfield and one outside it. Paragraph 1, section headings, and future-line titles must be navigable to both.

### 2. Research Statement, Not Personal Statement

CMU and Cornell are explicit: keep the spotlight on the ideas and research work, not the candidate as a person. Personal motivation can appear only as intellectual motivation: why these questions matter, how past work produced the future agenda, and what new knowledge will result.

**Cut:** childhood hooks, life story, emotional disclosure, diversity-statement content, teaching-philosophy content, and generic "I am passionate" claims.

### 3. Brand / North Star Before Details

MIT EECS calls this a **branding statement**; Caltech calls it a tagline / research story; CMU asks for the long-term agenda in the introduction. Before drafting sections, produce one sentence answering: **what kind of scholar will this committee remember?**

Good shapes:

- "My research develops [methods/tools/theory] that make [important class of systems/problems] [measurable outcome]."
- "I investigate [big problem] by combining [distinctive method] with [domain/resource], enabling [field-level consequence]."
- "My long-term goal is to [generate new knowledge / solve durable problem] by [specific approach]."

### 4. Story Logic: CMU + Caltech Moves

Use one of these moves to keep the statement coherent:

| Move | Best use | Required shape |
|---|---|---|
| **Sequence** (CMU) | Past/current/future arc is clear | Dissertation or postdoc work sets the stage for future work; each transition names what became possible next. |
| **Novelty** (CMU) | Candidate has a distinctive method, dataset, instrument, theory, or community | State the "secret weapon": what does this candidate have that others do not? |
| **Problem** (CMU) | Field gap is easy to name | Big problem → specific challenge → candidate's approach. |
| **ABT** (Caltech) | Opening, project pitch, or future-line summary | And: context. But: unresolved problem. Therefore: candidate's approach and impact. |

Do not mix all four visibly. Choose one dominant move; use others as local paragraph checks.

### 5. Past Work Builds Credibility, Not Chronology

US guidance consistently warns against over-explaining past experience. Use past work to prove future feasibility:

- Highlight 2–3 strongest results, not every paper.
- State the candidate's role in team work.
- Name evidence: publications, awards, grants, patents, deployed systems, citations, datasets, methods, preliminary results.
- Explain findings and significance, not just topics.
- Show how each past theme generates a future question.

If a past-work paragraph does not make a future line more credible, shorten or cut it.

### 6. Future Agenda Must Operate on Multiple Timelines

US schools vary in exact horizon: UConn stresses 1–3 years; Cornell and Yale often use 3–5 years; MIT and Harvard emphasize 5–10 years; Caltech asks for both short-term achievable goals and long-term vision. Synthesize these as:

1. **Get-go project** — what the first student or first year can start.
2. **Tenure-track project** — 2–3 concrete thrusts for years 1–6, with methods, outcomes, publications, and funding path.
3. **Long-term vision** — the 5–10 year field-level destination, not a finite project list.

Each future thrust must state: question, why it matters, approach, feasibility evidence from past work, likely outputs, funding/collaboration vector, and student/trainee role when relevant.

### 7. Fit Includes Institution Type, Resources, and Students

US fit is lighter than Dutch/EU fit, but it is not optional when the research statement stands alone. Cornell, Penn, Yale, Caltech, UConn, and Notre Dame all ask candidates to connect future work to institutional needs, facilities, goals, faculty, students, and resources.

Adjust fit by institution type:

| US target | What to emphasize |
|---|---|
| **R1 STEM / medical school** | Grant-readiness, labs/facilities, PhD/postdoc projects, centers, technical collaborators, startup feasibility. |
| **R1 humanities/social sciences** | Book/project trajectory, archives/data access, interdisciplinary centers, graduate advising, publication plan. |
| **Teaching-research / regional university** | Feasible scope with limited TA/postdoc support; undergraduate research projects; mission fit; manageable equipment needs. |
| **Primarily undergraduate institution** | Undergraduate research pipeline, student training, low-infrastructure projects, integration with teaching without turning the statement into a teaching statement. |

Never say you want to work with graduate students if the institution only has undergraduates.

### 8. Funding, Startup, and Resource Readiness

Cornell, CMU, MIT, Caltech, Yale, Penn, Notre Dame, and UConn all identify funding potential as a core signal. Include enough to show literacy, not a full budget.

For US targets, name 1–3 realistic funding vectors across the agenda:

- NSF CAREER / NSF core programs for many STEM and social-science fields.
- NIH K99/R00, R01, R21, K-series for biomedical / clinical / life sciences.
- DOE, DARPA, DoD, NASA, USDA, NEH, ACLS, Mellon, Sloan, Moore, Simons, ACS PRF, foundations, industry partners when field-appropriate.

For lab or equipment-heavy fields, the statement can mention required resources lightly; save detailed budget, lab space, equipment, and staffing plans for campus interviews or a longer research plan unless the brief asks.

### 9. Design Is Part of Persuasion

CMU, MIT, Caltech, Cornell, and other US career-center handouts all stress readability. Use design to make skim-reading possible:

- Informative headings that make claims, not labels: "Making Learned Systems Auditable" beats "Future Work".
- White space, short paragraphs, and occasional bullets for lists of aims or outputs.
- Bold only for load-bearing thesis sentences or aim titles.
- Figures only when they support a pivotal claim; they must be readable without subfield expertise.
- Avoid unexplained acronyms, current-lab shorthand, and jargon in headings.

### 10. Length Is US-Variable; The Brief Wins

US sources conflict because disciplines differ:

- CMU / Notre Dame / Cornell general guidance: often 1–3 pages.
- Yale / UConn: often 2–4 pages.
- MIT / Harvard / Caltech STEM: often 3–5 pages.
- Berkeley Math: can be longer, but use a 1-page accessible executive summary; over 10 pages is far too long.
- Cornell: a 5–15 page detailed version can be brought to interviews, not usually submitted unless requested.

When no page limit is given, choose by discipline and target: 3 pages is the safest US STEM default; 2 pages is safer for broad, non-R1, or teaching-research positions; 4–5 pages only when field norms support it.

### 11. Feedback and Maintenance

US guidance repeatedly recommends feedback from mentors and peers, including readers outside the subfield. CMU suggests treating the research statement as a living document like a teaching statement.

Before delivery, ask:

- Has one specialist checked technical credibility?
- Has one non-specialist checked readability and story?
- Has a faculty mentor checked discipline norms and length?
- Does the statement still match the current CV, papers, grants, and job ad?

## US Overlay Checklist

Run this after the universal rubric:

1. **Bottom-line first:** Does paragraph 1 state the research identity, problem, and future direction without throat-clearing?
2. **Ideas, not autobiography:** Is every personal sentence tied to research questions, methods, or future contribution?
3. **Mixed-reader accessible:** Could a department member outside the subfield summarize the agenda?
4. **Past → future logic:** Does each past-work theme justify a future thrust?
5. **2–3 concrete thrusts:** Are there named projects with methods, outputs, and horizons?
6. **Multiple timelines:** Is there a get-go project, a tenure-track plan, and a long-term vision?
7. **Funding/resource literacy:** Are realistic grants, partners, facilities, or startup needs acknowledged at the right level?
8. **Institution-specific fit:** Does the statement mention resources, centers, faculty, student population, or mission that only this institution has?
9. **Institution-type fit:** Are the proposed projects feasible for an R1, PUI, teaching-research, medical, or liberal-arts context as applicable?
10. **Readable design:** Do headings, whitespace, bullets, and figures help a busy committee skim?
11. **No orphan ideas:** Has every miscellaneous project that does not serve the brand been cut?
12. **Feedback-ready:** Has the draft been tested on specialist and non-specialist readers?
