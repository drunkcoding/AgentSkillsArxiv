## 1. Standard Structure (paragraph order)

A strong 1–2 page statement follows this skeleton:

| § | Section | Purpose | Length |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Opening hook** | Anchor the reader with a specific moment, metaphor, or question | 80–150 words |
| 2 | **Teaching philosophy / vision** | State your 2–4 core principles in *your* words, not jargon | 100–150 words |
| 3 | **Methods in action (×1–3)** | Each: principle → specific activity → outcome/evidence | 200–350 words |
| 4 | **Supervision / mentorship** | 1:1 and small-group work; explicit at TU Delft | 100–180 words |
| 5 | **Courses you can teach** | Map expertise to existing courses + 1–2 new courses you would develop | 80–150 words |
| 6 | **Closing / forward vision** | What you bring + how you will continue to grow | 60–120 words |

Total: ~600–1000 words = 1 to 1.5 single-spaced pages. **Three pages is the absolute ceiling**, and committee chairs report a strong preference for 1–2 pages even when more is allowed.

**Variant: TU Delft / EU "education vision".** Reorder slightly:
1. Hook → 2. Vision (sometimes labelled *onderwijsvisie*) → 3. Methods → 4. Supervision (often weighted heavier than US versions) → 5. Mapping to TU Delft programmes → 6. Closing.

## 2. Length & Word Budget

- **1 page** if vacancy says "typically one page" — write to the typical, not the cap.
- Single-spaced, 11–12 pt serif, ~1-inch margins.
- 600–700 words ≈ 1 page; 1000–1100 words ≈ 1.5 pages; 1500 words ≈ 2 pages.
- **Do not pad to fill space.** A tight one-pager beats a baggy three-pager.

## 3. Voice, Tense, Register

- **First person ("I")** throughout. This is a personal vision document, not a literature review.
- **Present tense** is the default ("I design my lectures around…"). Past tense for prior teaching ("When I taught COMP-101 in 2023…"). Future tense only for explicit plans ("I would develop a graduate seminar on…").
- **Discipline-aware but not jargon-heavy.** A non-CS dean on the search committee should follow every paragraph.
- **Confident, not boastful.** Replace "I am passionate about" with what you actually do.
- **Conversational professionalism**: write as if talking to a thoughtful colleague over coffee.

## 4. The "Show Don't Tell" Loop

Every claim should follow this 3-beat pattern:

> **Principle** → **Specific action** → **Outcome / evidence**

| Weak (tell) | Strong (show) |
|---|---|
| "I believe in active learning." | "After explaining cache eviction policies, I give pairs a memory trace and ask them to predict hits/misses before whole-class discussion. Last term, 78% predicted correctly after this activity, up from 34% the prior semester." |
| "I value student belonging." | "I learn the names of all 60 students in the first two weeks and offer a weekly 10-minute open slot — used most by quieter students whose questions never surface in lecture." |
| "I emphasise hands-on learning." | "In my web-architecture course, the semester project is a multi-tier service students deploy to a small VPS — 14 of 22 teams kept it running past the course end, with three becoming portfolio pieces in job applications." |

If a paragraph contains principles but no specifics, cut it or rewrite with one concrete example.

## 5. Opening Hooks That Work

Five reliable patterns (pick one, do not stack them):

1. **Personal anecdote / formative moment**
   > "The first time I taught recursion, half the room nodded; the other half stared. I had explained the call stack with words instead of with the stack itself drawn live on the board. The next lecture, I drew the frames and traced through `factorial(4)` slowly. That session became the template for how I now teach every abstraction in CS: build the mental machine in front of the students, then let them run it."

2. **Guiding question**
   > "When I prepare a class, I ask three questions: What is the one idea I want every student to leave with? What misconception do they likely arrive with? How will I see the misconception change in real time?"

3. **Concrete vision statement**
   > "My goal as an educator is to graduate computer scientists who debug their own thinking — who recognise when a model fails, instrument the gap, and update."

4. **Field-grounded metaphor**
   > "I think of teaching as incremental compilation: each new concept must link cleanly against everything the student already knows, or the build breaks."

5. **Stakes statement**
   > "Computer science now mediates every public institution. The students in our lecture halls will write the systems that decide who gets credit, who gets care, and what is read. My teaching is shaped by that responsibility."

**Avoid** generic openers ("Teaching is my passion"), quotes from famous educators in lieu of your own framing, and dictionary-definition openings.

## 6. Closing Strategies That Work

A strong close does **one** of these — not all of them:

1. **Forward vision tied to the institution.** Name 1–2 specific TU Delft courses you would teach or programmes you would contribute to.
2. **A growth statement.** Acknowledge a specific area you are still developing (e.g., assessment of large-cohort projects, integrating LLMs responsibly) — committees value reflective candidates.
3. **Bridge to research.** A one-sentence link from teaching back to your research agenda — signals coherence.
4. **A specific contribution promise.** What you uniquely bring that the department does not already have.

**Do not**:
- Restate the opening verbatim.
- End with "Thank you for your consideration" — the cover letter does that.
- Introduce a new principle in the final paragraph.

## 7. Course-Mapping Language

Use **modal language** ("I am prepared to…", "I would welcome the opportunity to…", "I could develop…") rather than declarative ("I will teach…"). The latter sounds presumptuous when a department already has someone teaching that course.

**Three-tier mapping** (use all three for credibility):

1. **Existing courses I am ready to teach now** — name them with course codes if known. Show you read the curriculum.
2. **Existing courses I could contribute to** — courses where you would join an existing teaching team.
3. **A new course I could develop** — propose 1, not 5. Tie it to your research and to a curriculum gap.

Example (CS-systems candidate at TU Delft):
> "From the BSc CSE curriculum I am prepared to teach **Operating Systems** and **Computer Networks**, and would welcome contributing to the second-year **Software Project**. In the MSc, I would teach into the **Computer Systems** theme — Real-time Systems, Modern Computer Architecture — and would propose developing a graduate elective on *Systems for Machine Learning* that bridges the MSc CS Computer Systems theme with the MSc DSAIT Advanced Machine Learning theme."

## 8. Cliches and Traps to Avoid

**Strip these phrases entirely:**
- "I am passionate about teaching" — say what you do instead
- "My door is always open" — every applicant says this
- "Students learn in different ways" — vague; if true, show *which* differences you actually accommodate
- "I want to prepare students for the real world" — what specifically?
- "I bring my research into the classroom" — show how, with one example
- "I create a safe space" — show structurally, e.g., anonymous question tools, mid-term feedback
- "Critical thinking" used more than once — treat it as a one-shot term

**Avoid these structural mistakes:**

1. **Listing courses without philosophy.** "I can teach X, Y, Z" is a CV bullet, not a statement. Embed the list in your reasoning.
2. **Theory parade.** Citing Foucault, Vygotsky, Freire, Bloom, and Biggs in two paragraphs is a red flag. Cite at most 1–2 frameworks you actually apply, and apply them concretely.
3. **Deficit framing of students.** "Today's students lack…" reads poorly. Speak about what students bring and how you build on it, then name structural barriers if relevant.
4. **Saccharine sentiments indistinguishable from any other applicant.** A statement that could be signed by anyone in your subfield is a failed statement.
5. **Reading like a research paper.** No abstract, no related-work section. Personal narrative and concrete teaching detail.
6. **Promising more than you can deliver.** "I will revolutionise the curriculum" with no specifics is a warning sign to committees.
7. **Hiding weak teaching evidence.** If your teaching record is thin, name it honestly and describe how you have prepared (TA experience, UTQ progress, teaching workshops, undergraduate supervision).
8. **Skipping supervision.** TU Delft (and most EU R1s) explicitly ask about it. Two paragraphs minimum.
9. **Avoiding inclusion / diversity.** Even brief, specific comments are stronger than silence. If you have a structural practice (anonymous early-term surveys, code-of-conduct in group work), name it.
10. **Generic AI/LLM stance.** If you discuss LLMs in teaching, take a specific position (ban / scaffold / require disclosure / use as study partner) tied to a course-level decision, not slogans.

## Quick Self-Review Checklist (before submission)

- [ ] Under 1.5 pages (or under the vacancy cap with margin)
- [ ] Opens with something only *I* would write
- [ ] Each principle paired with at least one specific example or numeric outcome
- [ ] Supervision section explicitly addresses 1:1 and small-group work
- [ ] At least three TU Delft course names (verified from studyguide.tudelft.nl)
- [ ] One sentence linking teaching to my research
- [ ] Reflective note about how I plan to grow
- [ ] No "passionate", no "open door", no Foucault
- [ ] First-person, mostly present tense
- [ ] Read aloud — every sentence still earns its place
