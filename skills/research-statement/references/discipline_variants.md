# Discipline-Specific Variations

The general rubric and structure apply across disciplines, but allocations, expectations, and key signals differ. Load only the section for your discipline.

## At-a-Glance Comparison

| Discipline | Length | Key Focus | Distinctive Elements | Past:Future ratio |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| CS / Engineering | 2–3 (EU) / 3–4 (US) | Technical contribution, fundability, fit | Algorithm/system specifics, industrial collab | 40:50 |
| Life Sciences (US R1) | 3–5 | NIH-shape, lab infra, preliminary data | Specific Aims style; equipment/budget hints | 35:55 |
| Chemistry | 3–5 | Executive summary + 3 prioritized projects | Top-section pitches readable independently | 30:60 |
| Physics | 3–5 | Big-context framing + experiments | Often by technique/instrument | 40:50 |
| Medicine / Biomedical | 3–5 | K-award shape; transition to independence | Mentor structure; clinical relevance | 30:60 |
| Mathematics | 4–6 | Mathematical taste; program of problems | Executive summary + technical detail | 50:40 |
| Theoretical CS | 3–5 | Single problem vs. program | Theorems-and-conjectures style | 50:40 |
| Social Sciences | 2–3 | Theoretical contribution; data access | Methodological tools; policy relevance | 40:50 |
| Economics | 2–3 | Identification strategy; data | Project-based; technical depth | 40:50 |
| Psychology | 2–3 | Subfield specificity; mentor match | Population/method specificity | 40:50 |
| Humanities (junior) | 2 (hard cap) | Book project arc; theoretical framing | Diss-to-book trajectory + second project | 60:30 |
| Humanities (senior) | 4–6 | Depth + breadth; cultural value | Multi-book trajectory | 60:30 |
| Law | 2–3 | Doctrinal contribution; policy relevance | Practical application | 40:50 |

## CS / Engineering

**Length:** 2–3 pages (EU/Dutch/UK), 3–4 pages (US R1). 5+ is risky everywhere.

**Section weighting (3-page US default):**
- Vision/opening: ~10%
- Past work (2–3 themes): ~35%
- Future research (2–3 lines): ~40%
- Fit + closing: ~15%

**What reviewers look for (weighted):**
1. **Vision clarity** — single sentence; subfield-recognizable.
2. **Future-research fundability** — NSF CAREER, NSF medium, NIH (for biomedical CS), NWO Veni/Vidi, ERC StG-shaped lines.
3. **Departmental fit** — at most US R1 light; heavy at Dutch/EU.
4. **Past-work evidence** — venue-specific (top-venue papers, citations, deployment).
5. **Independence** — hard signal; future direction must depart from PhD/postdoc.

**CS-specific opening patterns** (see `openings.md` for verbatim examples):
- "Today's [systems area] suffers from [specific gap]. My research [vision]." — Marcus, Hariharan
- "My goal is to [vision in one sentence]." — Westrick, Ringer
- "I work on understanding [specific intellectual territory]." — Chaudhari

**Distinctive expectations:**
- Name **specific systems / algorithms / theorems** by name. Reviewers parse for technical concreteness.
- Quantify everything: speedups, error rates, citations, deployments, scale (qubits, parameters, traffic).
- Industrial collaboration is **counted positively** in EU/Dutch (valorization) and increasingly in US.
- Open-source artifacts are reviewer-readable; mention the GitHub URL or system name.

**Top failure modes:**
1. Too high-level — "I work on AI" — gets ranked below candidates with named subfields.
2. No funding plan in EU/Dutch applications.
3. Past work organized by year instead of by theme.

## Life Sciences (Biology / Microbiology / Genetics / Neuroscience)

**Length:** 3–5 pages typical at US R1. 2–3 pages at EU/UK.

**Section weighting (4-page US default):**
- Vision: ~5%
- Background/motivation: ~15%
- Past-work / preliminary data: ~25%
- Specific Aims-style future: ~45%
- Fit + closing: ~10%

**What reviewers look for:**
1. **NIH/grant-readiness** — future research often shaped as 3 Specific Aims with hypotheses.
2. **Preliminary data** — at least one figure or quantitative result that supports feasibility of the future agenda.
3. **Lab infrastructure** — equipment needs, model organism, key reagents (signaling to dept what startup looks like).
4. **Collaboration network** — named collaborators with complementary expertise.
5. **Broader impacts** — for NIH-relevant work, clinical/translational potential.

**NIH K99/R00 transferable structure** (the most common future-research scaffold):
- Aim 1: Mechanism / characterization (safer)
- Aim 2: Functional / consequence (ambitious)
- Aim 3: Application / extension (high-risk-high-reward)

**Distinctive expectations:**
- One **figure** is often included (preliminary data or schematic). Acceptable if format permits.
- **Specific Aims style** is common: bolded aim title + 1-paragraph hypothesis + 1-paragraph approach.
- Reviewers check independence by asking: *what does the candidate bring that the postdoc lab doesn't already do?*

**Top failure modes:**
1. Future research that reads as continuation of postdoc lab's work — independence missing.
2. No preliminary data.
3. Cargo-cult NIH structure without genuine aim differentiation.

## Chemistry

**Length:** 3–5 pages with executive summary on first page.

**Distinctive structure:** Many chemistry departments expect an **executive summary** on page 1 listing 3 prioritized project ideas, followed by detailed plans.

> "Most of the committee will read the executive summary; specialists read the detailed plan." — common chemistry hiring practice

**Section weighting:**
- Executive summary (3 ideas, 1–2 sentences each): ~15%
- Detailed plans (3 projects): ~70%
- Fit + closing: ~15%

**Distinctive expectations:**
- 3 projects with clearly differentiated risk profile (safer / ambitious / high-risk).
- Synthesis route or instrumentation specificity for at least the safer project.
- Funding source for each project (NIH, NSF, ACS PRF, DOE).

## Physics

**Length:** 3–5 pages.

Often organized **by technique or experiment** rather than by theme. Includes "big context" framing — connecting your work to ongoing physics community priorities (e.g., Snowmass for particle, Astro2020 priorities for astrophysics).

**Distinctive expectations:**
- Mention specific facilities, instruments, or collaborations (LIGO, LHC, ALMA, JWST, ITER).
- Long-term vision aligned with discipline-wide priorities.
- Theory-experiment connection if applicable.

## Medicine / Biomedical

**Length:** 3–5 pages.

Strong K-award (K99/R00, K01, K08) influence. Career-development structure is common.

**Distinctive expectations:**
- Clinical relevance stated explicitly.
- Training plan rationale (what techniques will you learn / have you learned).
- Mentor/co-mentor structure indicated.
- Patient cohort / clinical data access named.

## Mathematics

**Length:** 4–6 pages. (Berkeley Math: "If your statement runs over ten pages, it is far too long. But 2 pages is distressingly short.")

**Distinctive structure:** Executive summary (1 page accessible to non-specialists) + detailed technical sections. Theorems are often stated.

**What reviewers look for:**
1. **Mathematical taste** — what kinds of problems excite you, what techniques you bring.
2. **Single problem vs. program** — is there a coherent program, or just a list of theorems?
3. **Big-picture vision** — connect specific results to the field's larger questions.
4. **Independence** — clear distinction from advisor's program is harder in math; signal through choice of problems and own conjectures.

**Distinctive expectations:**
- Theorems are stated formally (or informally with a precise problem statement).
- Conjectures and open problems are listed.
- Connections to other areas of math signal breadth.

## Theoretical CS

Similar to mathematics. 3–5 pages. Theorems and conjectures are stated. Single-problem-vs-program tension is similar.

## Social Sciences (Sociology / Political Science / Anthropology)

**Length:** 2–3 pages.

**What reviewers look for:**
1. **Theoretical contribution** — what theory does your work advance or challenge?
2. **Methodological tools** — qualitative, quantitative, mixed; specific techniques.
3. **Data access** — named datasets, archives, fieldwork plans.
4. **Policy relevance** — for political science, applied sociology, etc.
5. **Engagement with debates** — name the debate you are intervening in.

**Distinctive expectations:**
- Specific theoretical framing (named theorists, named debates).
- Replication, ethics, and IRB considerations may be mentioned.
- Mixed-methods candidates should explicitly justify the combination.

## Economics

**Length:** 2–3 pages.

**Distinctive expectations:**
- Project-based structure (each project: research question, identification strategy, data, expected outputs).
- Technical depth expected (model class, identification approach).
- Empirical or theoretical orientation made explicit.
- Data sources named (Census, administrative, proprietary, RCT).

## Psychology

**Length:** 2–3 pages.

**Distinctive expectations:**
- Subfield specificity (cognitive, social, clinical, developmental, cognitive neuroscience).
- Population / sample specificity (children, adults, clinical, cross-cultural).
- Methods specificity (fMRI, EEG, longitudinal, RCT, computational modeling).
- Mentor match for postdoc — explicit alignment.

## Humanities (Junior — Pre-Tenure / First Job)

**Length:** Karen Kelsky's hard rule: **2 pages**. Going beyond is "resented."

**Distinctive structure (5-paragraph model):**
1. Overview of the field and your intervention.
2. Dissertation summary (compressed).
3. The contribution your work makes.
4. The second book / next major project.
5. Articles, public scholarship, future arc.

**What reviewers look for:**
1. **Single coherent argument** — the book project arc.
2. **Theoretical framing** — named theorists, intellectual lineage.
3. **Archive / source access** — for historical / literary work.
4. **Second book** — non-negotiable; without it you're a "one-trick pony" (Kelsky).
5. **Publishing trajectory** — past articles, book contract status, planned articles.

**Distinctive expectations:**
- The diss-to-book trajectory must be addressed: timeline, target press, status.
- The second book signals research life beyond the dissertation.
- Public scholarship / podcast / op-ed work matters in some humanities subfields.

## Humanities (Senior / Lateral / Endowed Chair)

**Length:** 4–6 pages.

Multi-book trajectory; depth and breadth (REF-style) considerations; cultural heritage value.

## Law

**Length:** 2–3 pages.

**Distinctive expectations:**
- Doctrinal contribution clearly stated.
- Interdisciplinary methods (law and economics, law and society, empirical legal studies) named.
- Practical relevance / policy application.
- Course-teaching alignment with research often expected.
