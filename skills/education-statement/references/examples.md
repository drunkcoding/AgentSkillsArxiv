## 1. Anatomy of a Strong CS Statement

Across 8 successful CS / AI / Data Science faculty statements analysed, the shared structure is:

```
[Opening hook: anecdote / question / vision] — 1 paragraph (~120 words)
[Philosophy: 2–4 named principles] — 1 paragraph (~150 words)
[Methods in action: 1–3 course narratives] — 2–3 paragraphs (~300 words)
[Supervision / mentorship: 1–2 paragraphs] — (~150 words)
[Courses I could teach: 3-tier mapping] — 1 paragraph (~120 words)
[Closing: forward vision tied to institution] — 1 paragraph (~80 words)
```

Total ≈ 800–1000 words ≈ 1.5 single-spaced pages.

**Word-count distribution among successful statements**: shortest ~600 words (one tight page), longest ~1200 words. The 800–1000 range is the sweet spot.

## 2. Eight Real CS Faculty Statements

For each: author, position at time of writing, core stance, structure, and a verbatim opening or closing.

### Example 1 — Cameron Allen (UC Berkeley postdoc / RL)
- **Core stance**: AI as learning infrastructure — fascinated by moments of insight; teaching as generative collaboration.
- **Structure**: Opening motivation → teaching experience → four pillars (Creativity, Curiosity, Compassion, Context) → future teaching → mentoring.
- **Opening**:
  > "I am drawn to the field of artificial intelligence by my fascination with learning and teaching. The precise moments of clear, hard-won insight that arise after sufficient exposure to a new concept are some of the most gratifying experiences of my life, whether I notice them in my own mind or in the minds of my students."

### Example 2 — Deepak Narayanan (Stanford PhD, systems × ML)
- **Core stance**: Communication as impact multiplier — teaching as a way to broaden audience for research.
- **Structure**: Brief intro → TA experience (recitation design, course creation) → courses I could teach → new-course proposal at the systems–ML intersection.
- **Length**: ~1 page (very tight).
- **Closing (course proposal)**:
  > "Additionally, I would love to introduce a class focused on the intersection of systems and machine learning that discusses the practical issues (e.g., efficient model training and inference, model monitoring, training data collection) associated with learning and deploying models in production."

### Example 3 — Azza Abouzied (NYU Abu Dhabi, Associate Prof, databases)
- **Core stance**: Instructional scaffolding + inclusive learning pathways; experimental mindset toward course design.
- **Structure**: Philosophy (scaffolding + inclusivity) → three developed courses (OS, Database Systems, Data) → approaches that worked → mentorship.
- **Opening**:
  > "As a researcher by training, I approach teaching with an experimental mindset, trying out different lecturing techniques, books, case studies, etc., and exploring different ways of addressing externalities such as the shift to remote learning during COVID, the impact of AI, and the constantly evolving field of computer science."

### Example 4 — Osbert Bastani (UPenn, Associate Prof, ML)
- **Core stance**: Coverage in a way students might have discovered it themselves — context + intuition before formalism.
- **Structure**: Two course narratives (applied ML + trustworthy ML seminar) → teaching methodology evolution → research advising.
- **Opening**:
  > "My goal in teaching is to cover material in a way that conveys how the students might have discovered the material themselves. This approach means conveying both the broader context in which the problem is interesting, as well as the core intuition or picture behind algorithms and proofs."

### Example 5 — Pratik Chaudhari (UPenn, Associate Prof, ML/optimisation)
- **Core stance**: Making the intangible tangible; online–physical course parity; deep engagement.
- **Structure**: Course development narrative (ESE 546 Deep Learning) → evolution of teaching methodology → mentoring philosophy → undergraduate research.
- **Mentorship excerpt**:
  > "I believe in letting the student drive the research. I give high-level feedback rather than prescribe specific directions. This approach builds confidence, ownership, and the ability to think independently."

### Example 6 — Ramakrishna Vedantam (research scientist → faculty market, ML)
- **Core stance**: Slow + fast + group learning balance.
- **Structure**: Philosophy → graduate TA experience → undergraduate TA experience → mentorship → future teaching interests.
- **Three-pillar excerpt**:
  > "Deep Exploration: Leveraging the whiteboard to dive into topics with depth and clarity, fostering a 'slow-learning' approach that builds a strong conceptual foundation. Hands-On Engagement: Encouraging 'fast-learning' through interactive exploration and programming exercises that bring key concepts to life. Collaborative Growth: Facilitating 'group learning' through team-based projects that promote peer collaboration, communication, and shared discovery."

### Example 7 — Yining Liu (job market, CS, Harvard teaching award)
- **Core stance**: Lecturer as guide on intellectual journey; effective lectures, safe environment, genuine excitement.
- **Structure**: Teaching philosophy → three pillars → mentoring → future course interests.
- **Opening**:
  > "In my perspective, the role of a lecturer resembles a guide on students' intellectual journey. As a result, I strive to give effective lectures, create a safe learning environment, and make students genuinely excited to learn."

### Example 8 — Zhu-Tian Chen (Harvard Teaching Fellow, HCI/visualisation)
- **Core stance**: Stage with students as protagonists; inclusion through empathy + explicit learning objectives + progressive scaffolding.
- **Structure**: Philosophy (3 principles) → TA experience at HKUST (COMP2711, 90 students) → mentorship (18 students) → future courses.
- **Excerpt on explicit objectives**:
  > "I always started by telling the students what they were expected to learn and ended by telling them what I had taught. For each question, I further explained its importance, such as telling the students how it connected to the knowledge in the textbook and how necessary to understand it for the exams."

### Example 9 — Talia Ringer (UIUC, programming languages)
- **Core stance**: Building knowledge, enthusiasm, curiosity, and independence.
- **Structure**: Goal statement → teaching (classroom, specific courses) → advising (1-on-1 philosophy, distinct PhD/MSc/BSc styles).
- **Mentoring stance**:
  > "My advising philosophy is to communicate well with my students, learn from them, and give them the tools that empower them to succeed as independent researchers. Rather than choose a project for a student, I prefer to work with them to design a project they are passionate about, since I believe students thrive most when they are passionate."

## 3. Common Structural Patterns Observed

| Pattern | When it works | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **"Three pillars"** | When you have 3 distinct, applied principles | Sounds formulaic if pillars aren't truly distinct |
| **Single course case-study** | When you have one course you redesigned thoroughly | Insufficient breadth signal if you only have one course |
| **Two course narratives + supervision + future** | When you have 2+ course experiences | The most balanced — recommended default |
| **Vision-led (philosophy first, examples second)** | When you have strong vision but lighter teaching record | Risks abstraction without specifics |
| **Experience-led (examples first, philosophy emerges)** | When you have rich teaching record | Risks reading like a CV bullet list |

**Recommended default for TU Delft**: *two course narratives + supervision + future* — balances depth, breadth, and the supervision emphasis the prompt requests.

## 4. Three TU Delft–Oriented Skeleton Outlines

### Skeleton A — ML / AI candidate

**Hook**: A specific moment teaching a deep-learning concept (e.g., gradient flow), where you saw a student's mental model click — paired with the technique that triggered it.

**Philosophy** (3 principles):
1. *Build intuition before formalism* — visualisation, toy examples, dimensional analysis before equations.
2. *Make ML's failure modes visible* — students debug a real model's failure each week, not just train one to convergence.
3. *Responsibility integrated, not bolted on* — fairness/privacy/safety appear in the assignment that introduces the technique, not in a final ethics lecture.

**Methods (2 paragraphs)**: One on a graduate ML course you taught (specific technique + outcome). One on a project/lab innovation (e.g., a reproducibility checklist students must satisfy on every assignment).

**Supervision**: Scaffolding-and-fading framework with one anonymised MSc thesis outcome.

**Courses**: "From the MSc DSAIT core, I would teach **Machine and Deep Learning** and **Software Engineering and Testing for AI Systems**. I would contribute to the **Advanced Machine Learning** theme. From the BSc CSE I am prepared to teach **Machine Learning** in the second-year core. I would propose developing an MSc-level seminar on *Reproducible Machine Learning Research* that bridges DSAIT's research course with practical research training."

**Closing**: One-sentence link from teaching to research (e.g., "My research on [X] feeds the lab and the classroom in the same direction…") + a growth note.

### Skeleton B — Software Engineering / Systems candidate

**Hook**: A moment from a project course where a student team's design choice failed in production-like conditions and what they learned.

**Philosophy** (3 principles):
1. *Engineering judgement is taught through real artefacts* — students ship working systems, not problem sets only.
2. *Quality-as-pedagogy* — testing, code review, version control are pedagogical structures, not afterthoughts.
3. *Constructive Alignment is the planning discipline* — every assignment ladders to an ILO and an assessable artefact.

**Methods**: One paragraph on a project course you led (with an external client if possible — fits CDIO). One on assessment design (specifications grading, code review as graded artefact, or auto-grader pitfalls you have addressed).

**Supervision**: Lab-meeting framing with weekly group meeting + 1:1 hybrid; scaling from BSc to PhD.

**Courses**: "From the BSc CSE I am prepared to teach **Software Quality and Testing** (CSE1110), the **Collaborative Software Engineering Project** (CSE1105), and would welcome leading a section of **CSE2000 Software Project**. From the MSc CS I would teach **Software Architecture** and contribute to the **Software Engineering** theme. I would propose developing an MSc-level course on *Engineering Reliability for ML-Enabled Systems* — a curriculum gap as DSAIT graduates increasingly enter ML-engineering roles."

**Closing**: Tie to TU Delft's CDIO heritage and the Software Project sequence; one growth note.

### Skeleton C — Theoretical CS / PL / Algorithms candidate

**Hook**: A moment teaching a proof technique or invariant where you watched students transition from "following the proof" to "having the proof".

**Philosophy** (3 principles):
1. *Proof is a mental movement, not a static artefact* — students rehearse the movement out loud, in pairs.
2. *Failure is the curriculum* — wrong proofs, broken algorithms, miscomputed bounds are where understanding consolidates.
3. *Computational thinking ≠ programming* — abstraction, decomposition, invariants, pre/post-conditions are taught as primary skills.

**Methods**: One paragraph on a discrete-math / algorithms / PL course (specific technique — e.g., Parsons-style proof problems). One on assessment innovation (specifications grading; mastery-based exams; oral defence of a proof).

**Supervision**: Written-feedback culture, mid-project memos, scaling BSc → MSc → PhD.

**Courses**: "From the BSc CSE I would teach **Reasoning & Logic** (CSE1300), **Algorithms and Data Structures** (CSE1305), and **Theoretical Foundations of CS**. From the MSc CS I would teach **Formal Reasoning about Software** (core) and contribute to the **Algorithmics** and **Programming Languages** themes. I would propose an MSc seminar on *Verified Systems Programming* that draws on the new generation of practical proof assistants."

**Closing**: Tie to research-led teaching at the MSc level; growth note about supervising larger BSc cohorts.

## Final Reminder

These skeletons are scaffolds, not templates to fill in. **The single strongest predictor of a successful statement is whether the writer's specific voice is audible.** The three skeletons above show *structure*; the writing must remain unmistakably yours.
